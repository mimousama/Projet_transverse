Email Phishing

The risk: A stranger poses as a relative in distress to obtain an urgent wire transfer.

  • How it works: The scammer sends the same message to thousands of people, hoping that one of them will have an account with the impersonated organization. They use official logos and an alarmist tone to pressure you into clicking a link that leads to a fake mirror site.

  • For example: You receive an email from "Health Insurance" claiming that your new health insurance card is ready. You are asked to click a button to confirm your address and pay a €2 shipping fee. If you do, the hacker retrieves your bank details and your address for future thefts.

What you must absolutely not do:

  • Never click on a link in an email that asks for your bank details or passwords.

  • Do not trust the sender's name; always check if the email address looks suspicious (e.g., taxes@gmail.com instead of .gov).

  • Do not open attached invoices if you are not expecting them; they often hide spyware programs.

Be careful: a similar email or one using the name of a relative may be used.
